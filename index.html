TREATY 4

OVERVIEW

Treaty 4, also known as the Qu'Appelle Treaty, was signed on September 15, 1874 at Fort Qu'Appelle. It covers a large area of southern Saskatchewan and parts of western Manitoba. Treaty 4 was one of the early Numbered Treaties signed between First Nations and the Government of Canada. It was negotiated during a time when Canada was expanding westward and settlers were beginning to move into the Prairie regions. The treaty allowed the Canadian government to gain access to land while establishing agreements with the Indigenous peoples who lived there.

WHO SIGNED THE TREATY

Several Indigenous groups were involved in signing Treaty 4. These included the Cree (Nehiyawak), Saulteaux (Ojibway), and Assiniboine (Nakoda) Nations. Some First Nations communities in Manitoba that are connected to Treaty 4 include Rolling River First Nation, Keeseekoowenin Ojibway First Nation, and Tootinaowaziibeeng Treaty Reserve (Valley River First Nation). These groups were directly affected by the treaty and the agreements that were made with the Canadian government. Treaty 4 was also signed by representatives of both the Canadian government and First Nations leaders. On behalf of Canada, the treaty was signed by Treaty Commissioner Alexander Morris along with other federal representatives. On behalf of First Nations, the treaty was signed by several chiefs and headmen from the Cree, Saulteaux, and Assiniboine Nations. One of the notable leaders involved in the treaty negotiations was Chief Piapot.
